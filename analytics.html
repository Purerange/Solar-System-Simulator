The x-axis depicts the relative average distance (semi-major axis of the approximated ellipse) of each planet/dwarf planet from the Sun.

The y-axis shows the inclination of each planet's/dwarf planet's orbital plane. The dwarf planets have particularly high inclinations since they are more readily perturbed by other bodies. The gas giants' are in concordance with the barycenter plane that they contribute to creating.

Jupiter dominates the mass contribution of the planets; it has more than 250% the combined mass of all other planets. The gas giants, primarily Jupiter, contributes to a Solar System barycenter that usually lies outside of the Sun. at an average of 1.07 solar radius from the center of the Sun.

Apparently magnitude: How bright an object is from an observer on Earth

Absolute magnitude: Measure of an object's inherent luminosity

Due to historical reasons, the more negative the value the brighter the object, and the star Vega was chosen as the base reference for absolute magnitude (Vega had an absolute magnitude of 0.0 at the time)

m - M = -5 * log(d) - 5
where m is apparent magnitude, M is absolute magnitude, and d is distance per 10 parsecs

This plot shows the evolution of the Sun's radius and luminosity over time. Stars spend most of its life (the 'adult' phase) on the main sequence, when it's burning hydrogen. The sun has been burning hydrogen in its core to produce helium for over 4.5 billion years and will continue to do so for another 5+ billion years.

The Sun will then expand to become a red giant, rapidly increasing in size and luminosity; the surface temperature drops, however, reflecting the optical shift to red. As a red giant, the Sun has consumed Mercury and Venus and has nearly reached Earth.

The degenerate helium solar core will eventually get dense and hot enough ignite the runaway triple-alpha process where 3 helium nucleus will fuse to form carbon. The helium core flash at this precise moment produces luminosity on the order of an entire galaxy's.

The Sun will shrink down significantly and once again start fusion in its core, this time with helium into carbon. Earth would also be consumed this time around when the Sun becomes a red giant again.

Once helium is exhausted, the degenerate carbon-oxygen core will not reach values to ignite carbon fusion. Instead, the Sun will start to expel its outer layers in thermal pulses, producing a nebula and leaving behind a carbon-oxygen white dwarf core as its final form.
